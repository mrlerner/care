For someone with severe ME/CFS

The specific things that helped people get better

These are real people who were very ill with the same illness you have — some as ill as you are right now: bedbound, tube-fed, in dark and silent rooms. They are not made up. Doctors and the people themselves wrote these stories down, and every source is listed at the bottom of this page. Some recovered fully. Some are still climbing back. Each one is a door that opened. Maybe one is a door for you too.

Hedda started with the very smallest movements — lying down

Hedda got sick at twelve. For most of the next seven years she was bedridden — lying still in a darkened room, fed through a tube in her nose, too ill to eat or sit up on her own. And then she got better. Here is exactly how it began — not with anything big, but with the smallest things, done from her bed.

She started by just moving one arm. Then turning herself over, because she'd been lying on her back. Tiny movements she chose herself, when she felt ready for them.

What Hedda actually did

From there, with help: opening the curtains a little to let in some light. Sitting up in bed with support. A few steps while someone held her. Sitting outside on the terrace. Each new thing added only after the one before it felt safe.

One of the first times the therapists drew the curtains and helped her sit up, she saw the faces of her family — people she hadn't been able to look at in years. She later described the whole slow journey back in words that say it all:

"It was like rising from the grave … to get my life back."

The tiny movements you've already been trying on your own — pumping your toes, rocking your pelvis — are the very kind of thing Hedda began with. You're already closer to her path than you know.

Hedda's story and her words come from a peer-reviewed study of young women who recovered from severe, bedbound ME/CFS. Krabbe et al., 2022

Cornelia found one simple rule — and it guided everything

Cornelia got better, and what helped her most was a single, clear test she could use every day.

Cornelia's rule

Do a small amount of something gentle. Then notice: do you wake up the next morning feeling okay? If yes — that's your safe level. Stay there for a while. Only when it feels easy do you add a tiny bit more.

For her, the gentle "something" was very slow movement — slow yoga or Pilates, done a little at a time, always staying on the safe side of feeling worse the next day. She also kept a steady rhythm to her days: regular meals, balancing rest and activity, and protecting her energy from things — and people — that drained her.

She had to learn her own body and her own limits, and trust them — and in time, the world opened back up.

"I learned to listen to my body … and it went well."

Cornelia's story is from the same peer-reviewed study. Krabbe et al., 2022

Dan was very ill — and his exact recipe was tiny and patient

Dan — his name is Dan Moricoli — was so ill he lived in a reclining chair for weeks at a time and couldn't walk the few steps to the bathroom without a cane. He had crashes so deep he couldn't be woken from them. With a specialist doctor's help, he recovered. And he wrote down his exact recipe — so you can see precisely how small the beginning was.

Dan's exact starting routine

Two five-minute walks a day, with a 10–20 minute rest in between — and only every other day. He held there for two full months before adding any more. Whenever he felt even a little tired, he rested right away — three or four times a day, twenty minutes at a time, or a real nap. Later, he wore a heart-rate monitor so he'd never accidentally push too hard.

That was it. No miracle drug, no extreme effort. Just patience, rest before he got tired, and the smallest steps — and a heart-rate monitor to make sure he never once pushed past his limit. His whole philosophy was two words, in capital letters:

"Pacing IS everything."

What's hopeful here isn't the walking — that comes much later, and only when the body is ready. It's how gentle, patient, and ordinary the recipe is. Every single step stayed inside a strict limit, never pushing past it. This is a path, not a leap. Years on, Dan was nearly fully recovered.

Dan Moricoli's recovery, under the care of Dr. Nancy Klimas, was documented in detail. Health Rising, 2015

Whitney was as ill as it is possible to be — and he is climbing back

This one is honest in a different way. Whitney has been as severely ill as anyone with this illness has ever been written about: bedbound for years, unable to speak at all, fed through a line into his body because he couldn't eat or drink, unable to bear light, sound, or even another person in the room.

He is not all the way better — he is still very ill. But he is improving. After years of patient searching, his doctors found a medicine that helped, and the gains held. He can write again now, for a little while most days.

"I got to enjoy things like seeing the sky for the first time in six years."

The most important thing he wanted people like you to know: through all of it, his mind stayed whole. The real Whitney was always in there. You are still you, completely — and improvement can come even from the very deepest place.

Whitney Dafoe wrote his own account in a peer-reviewed medical journal. Dafoe, 2021 · whitneydafoe.com

Jennifer's illness turned out to have a specific cause — and it could be fixed

Jennifer was largely confined to bed and used a wheelchair to leave the house. On a bad stretch she could manage only a few hundred steps in a whole day.

Her doctors eventually found something specific: a physical problem in her neck and spine that some people develop after a serious infection. It could be operated on. After the surgery, her symptoms lifted in a way she could hardly believe.

"I can hardly believe it myself. My ME is in remission."

She is careful to say this was her cause, not everyone's, and surgery is a serious step. But her story is a real, hopeful reminder: it's worth doctors looking carefully for a specific, treatable cause — because sometimes there is one.

Jennifer Brea, the filmmaker behind the documentary Unrest, wrote about her remission. Her own account · Health Rising

And recovery can be complete — the care that works is yours

A medical study followed fourteen people who had been completely bedbound with this illness — needing help with everything, some living in darkness and silence. Every one of them recovered, fully. When the doctors checked back years later, they were still well, still living their lives. One of them, a young man, had spent fifteen months in total darkness and silence, wearing a blindfold and ear coverings — and he recovered too.

And in Norway there is a unit built for exactly this. They keep the rooms quiet and the light low, help with everything, feed gently — through a tube or a line when needed — and never push. Of the very ill people they cared for, half got better, and not one got worse. The things that helped them are the very things being done for you every day: a calm room, protected rest, careful feeding, and small steps only when your body is ready.

You are already on the path that helps.

The fourteen recoveries: Bakken et al., 2023. The Norway care unit (Røysumtunet): Gundersen Storla et al., 2025.

These are real, specific things that brought real people back.

  • The smallest movements count. Moving an arm, turning over, pumping your toes — this is how Hedda began.
  • The next-day test. If a small thing doesn't leave you worse the next day, it was within your safe level. That's your compass.
  • Rest before you're tired, not after. Dan rested the moment he felt even slightly tired — many times a day.
  • Calm the alarm. Picture a peaceful place, say kind things to yourself, let the worry settle. Peace itself gives energy back.
  • Protect the quiet. The dark, the calm, the rest — these aren't nothing. They're the medicine working.
  • Add only a little, only when steady. Stay at each level until it feels easy. There's no rush, and a hard day is never the end of the story.
