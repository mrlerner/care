Prioritized Tracking Documents — Caregiver Printouts

A prioritized list of the paper printouts the patient's caregivers need. Paper system only — no software. Tier 1 maps directly to the three hospital-admission triggers (see hospital-triggers.md); Tiers 2–3 build the daily-care picture and the eventual cause-and-effect analysis. This is a list of what we need, not the form designs.

Tier 1 Hospital-trigger safety (print first)

These map directly to the three admission numbers in hospital-triggers.md.

  1. 1Daily Food & Fluid Log — captures intake (calories) and fluid-in / urine-out on one page; feeds the fluid-balance trigger and the nutrition picture at once. The workhorse sheet.

    Call the doctor if

    • Dehydration / fluid balance: urine output drops to roughly <0.5 mL/kg/hr (or simply far less than prior days); Total In − Urine Out stays negative day after day; or signs of dehydration appear — dark/scant urine, dry mouth, poor skin turgor.
    • Calories / intake: intake runs far below the daily target despite best effort across the day; she can't take any nutrition or fluid; or there's repeated vomiting.

    Exact cutoffs to be set by Dr. Friedly — these are the framework ranges from hospital-triggers.md.

  2. 2Weight Log — the weight-trend trigger; one row per weigh-in.

    Call the doctor if

    • Weight keeps falling despite feeding efforts.
    • Rapid loss — roughly ~1–2% of body weight in a week, or ~5% in a month.
    • A flat or falling trend even while intake looks adequate on paper (points to an absorption or fluid-loss problem).

    It's the trend that triggers a call, not any single weigh-in. Exact cutoffs to be set by Dr. Friedly.

  3. 3Lab / Electrolyte Tracker — the electrolyte trigger (phosphate / magnesium / potassium); a sheet to transcribe and trend blood-draw results, since these can't be measured at the bedside.

    Call the doctor if

    • Phosphate, potassium, or magnesium is falling across serial draws — especially in the 12–72h after a calorie increase (the refeeding window).
    • Any of the trio is severely low, or sodium is low/falling.

    A downward trend matters even before a value is critically low. Exact cutoffs to be set by Dr. Friedly.

Supporting printouts for the Daily Food & Fluid Log

They're what make it usable — print alongside Tier 1.

  • Recipe Cards — one per standard blend, so calories are looked up, never calculated at the bedside.
  • Reference Key (laminate, on the wall) — vessel volumes, quarters thresholds (≥75% good · 50–75% watch · ≤50% flag), the "behind before bedtime" rule, urine-color dehydration strip.

Tier 2 Start now / can't be backfilled / actively changing

  1. 4Symptom Sheet — the family's directed questions (sleep, thirst, hunger, new pain, primary complaint, hot spots, feet…); the richest daily input and impossible to reconstruct later, so start collecting immediately.
  2. 5Repositioning / Turn Chart — 2-hourly left / right / back; pressure-injury safety and a possible PEM factor.
  3. 6Medication Administration Record + Taper Tracker — they're actively de-escalating meds now; without this the reverse trial-of-one is uninterpretable.

Tier 3 Exposure tracking & routine

  1. 7Exertion Log — passive range-of-motion + self-initiated micro-movements (toe pumps, knee windshield wipers, pelvic rocking, small repositionings); the exposure side of the 12–48h PEM puzzle.
  2. 8Weekly Hygiene & Device-Break Checklist — bedding / shirt / pillowcase changes; neck-brace, eye-mask, and earplug off-time.
  3. 9Crash / PEM Event Marker — a simple way to flag a crash on the timeline so exertion can later be correlated against it.

Summary

9 printouts, plus the 2 supporting items (Recipe Cards, Reference Key) under the Daily Food & Fluid Log.

  • Tier 1 is the safety floor and serves the nutritionist appointment.
  • Tiers 2–3 build the daily picture and the eventual cause-and-effect (PEM) analysis.
