Gentle ways to calm the nervous system

Neuroplasticity approaches you can use from bed

Just the words — no background, only what to say out loud. Say one of these slowly, with a pause between each sentence. Stop whenever they've had enough; any single one can be the whole visit.

Gentle things to do — eyes closed

"Let's try a breathing exercise. Make the out-breath a little longer than the in-breath. We'll do it together until you want to rest. This is the most direct way to tell your body it's safe."

"Let's visit one calm memory together. Bring to mind a peaceful moment, and rest in just one detail of it — the warmth of the sun, a soft texture, a familiar smell. Stay with that single detail. This gentle picturing is the heart of every program."

"Let's rest your attention on one kind thing you can feel. The weight of the blanket, a spot of warmth, the quiet rise and fall of your breath. Notice it with a little curiosity, instead of scanning for what's wrong. There's nothing to fix — just rest there."

"Let's say a few quiet words in your mind. Something like: 'I'm safe enough right now. This moment is okay.' You don't have to believe it — just let it be the only thought. It's a soft way to quiet the alarm when worry rises."

"Let's bring to mind one small good thing. It can be tiny — a person, a sip of water, a moment of quiet. Hold it for just a flicker, then let it go. A small thank-you settles the body too."

"Let's let your mind follow something familiar. Walk slowly through a story you know well, a favorite place, or something gentle you've done a hundred times. Just drift along the easy, known part. Resting on something familiar gives the racing part a break."

If their head hurts

"It sounds like your head is doing the heavy kind of thinking. That's the sorting-and-solving kind, and it makes the hurt worse. Let's set all of it down for now. We'll let your head rest."

"Let's stay with the soft kind of thinking for a while. That's just remembering and picturing — nothing to work out or decide. It's far gentler on a sore brain. We don't need anything harder than that right now."

"All the decisions and plans can wait. I will hold every bit of that for you, and write it down so nothing gets lost. None of it has to live in your head right now. You can let your mind go quiet."

If their mind is racing or anxious

"Let's set the big questions down for a little while. You don't have to figure any of this out right now. The way out isn't a thought you can reach from bed today — it comes slowly, with rest and with help. For now, your only job is to rest."

"That racing feeling is just the alarm going off. It's your body trying to protect you, not something you have to obey. You're allowed to let it go quiet for a while. We can sit with it together until it settles."

"We will work on all of this later, together. Not today, and not from this bed — and never on your own. I'm going to help carry it. Right now there is nothing you need to solve."

Always okay to say

"We'll only ever do a tiny bit, and then we rest. There is no rush, and nothing we have to finish. Stopping early is the goal, not giving up. You can never do this wrong."

"You can stop me at any moment, for any reason. Just one word — 'stop' — is a whole sentence, and I'll go quiet right away. You never have to explain. Listening to your body is exactly the right thing to do."

"If anything starts to feel worse, we stop right away. Nothing here is worth pushing through for. The gentlest version is always the right one. Your comfort matters more than any of this."

You asked a good question about brain-retraining programs: is there anything you can do while you're still in bed and can't watch videos or listen much? Yes. The screens and audio are just the wrapping. The thing that actually does the work is small, quiet, and can be done lying down with your eyes closed.

The one rule that comes first

Thinking, feeling, and even listening all use the same energy as moving. They can bring on a crash the same way. So every single thing on this page is meant to be tiny — a few seconds to a minute or two — and then you stop and rest.

Stop before you feel tired, not after. The nervous system learns calm from gentleness and repetition, never from effort. If something makes you feel worse, you let it go — no guilt, no pushing. This is the opposite of "try harder."

And if a conversation or any sound suddenly becomes too much, it is always okay to stop right then — mid-sentence, no explanation needed. "I need to stop now" is a complete sentence. That's your body protecting you, not rudeness.

When your mind won't stop spinning

For when you're turning it over and over, trying to find a way out.

When you're very ill, the mind often races — searching for the answer, the exit, the thing that will fix this. That searching is one of the most natural things in the world. It is also, quietly, the alarm doing its job: a nervous system on high alert, trying to protect you. And like any alarm, it burns energy and keeps the body tense — which is the very thing we're trying to ease.

Here is the gentle, freeing part: you do not have to solve this right now. The way out is not a thought you can reach by thinking harder from bed today. Setting the problem down for a while isn't giving up — it's taking the load off, which is its own kind of healing. The figuring-out will still be there later, and you'll meet it with more to work with, and with people beside you.

When the spinning starts, you don't have to argue with it. You gently give the mind something softer to rest on instead — one of the small things below. That is what "distraction" really means here: not ignoring anything, just letting the tired, racing part of your mind set the problem down and rest.

Things you can do with your eyes closed

No screen, no sound. Pick one. A few seconds is plenty.

  • Slow it down on the out-breath. A few breaths where you let the out-breath be a little longer than the in-breath. This is the most direct way to tell your body it's safe to rest. Even three or four breaths count — this is the foundation under every program.
  • Visit one calm memory. Bring to mind one peaceful moment and rest in a single detail of it — warmth of the sun, a soft texture, a familiar smell. This gentle picturing is the core tool inside the big programs, just without the course around it.
  • Rest your attention on one kind sensation. The weight of the blanket, a spot of warmth, the quiet rise and fall of your breath. Notice it with a little curiosity, rather than scanning for what's wrong.
  • A quiet phrase, said only in your mind. Something like "I'm safe enough right now. This moment is okay." A soft way to interrupt the alarm when worry rises. Nothing to hear, nothing to read.
  • One small thank-you. Bring to mind one small good thing — a person, a sip of water, a moment of quiet. Just a flicker. Then let it go.
  • Follow something familiar. Walk slowly through a story you know well, a favorite place, or the steps of something gentle you've done a hundred times. Letting the mind rest on something easy and known gives the racing part a break — this is gentle distraction, and it counts.

Some kinds of thinking cost less than others

Not all thinking is equally hard on a tender brain.

When the brain is inflamed and sore, it can feel a little like the weeks after a concussion: some kinds of thinking are gentle, and some make the hurt worse. Often the soft kinds are the familiar, story-shaped ones — remembering, picturing, listening to something kind. The harder kinds are the ones that make the brain work: figuring things out, making decisions, holding numbers or plans in mind, taking in new and complicated information.

So when your mind hurts, it isn't that you're thinking "wrong" — you may just be doing the heavy kind. It is completely okay to put the heavy thinking down for now: the decisions, the planning, the searching for answers. Those can wait for a steadier day, or for the people helping you to carry for a while. The gentle kinds are always allowed.

If a little sound feels okay one day

Only on a good moment — and you can stop at any second.

If listening is bearable for a short while, a very short guided practice with a soft voice, eyes closed, can do the same work — as long as you can pause or stop the instant it's too much. This includes gentle guided relaxation or hypnosis recordings, which simply walk your nervous system into a calmer state. The Curable and Gupta programs also have brief audio meditations. Treat all of this as completely optional; the silent practices above are enough on their own.

Things the people caring for you can help with too

You don't have to do all of this alone, or from memory.

Beyond what you can do from bed, care teams often add other gentle, body-based approaches — things like acupuncture, or coaching from a rehabilitation-psychology team who work with pacing and with living alongside a hard illness. These take a little participation, so they're for when you're somewhat steadier — but they exist, they're a normal part of care, and they'll be there for you when the time is right. Until then, the quiet breaths are plenty.

One honest note

These approaches are genuinely debated among people with ME/CFS. Some find them calming and helpful; others dislike the "it's your brain" framing, and there's a real risk if a program ever nudges someone to push past their limits to "retrain faster."

So the safe version, for you, is simple: a gentle experiment that lives entirely inside your energy envelope. Never a replacement for rest, pacing, your medical care, or nutrition — and dropped the moment it costs more than it gives.
