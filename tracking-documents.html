Daily Care Forms

Simple forms for the patient's caregivers. Fill them in by hand. Each form tells you what to do — there is no math, and you can't go wrong by writing things down.

⚠️ These are organizing forms — not medical advice. The doctor and dietitian decide the patient's food amounts and any medicine changes. Important: do not increase food amounts quickly — the doctor sets how fast to add calories.

Quick Guide (put on the wall)

Print this, cover it in plastic, and put it on the wall. It helps everyone fill in the sheets the same way.

Helper sheet

✅ How much did the patient take?

¾ or more = good About half = keep an eye out Half or less = tell the nurse
  • 0 / ¼ / ½ / ¾ / all = none / quarter / half / three-quarters / all
  • Write what the patient took, not what you gave.
  • Half or less, several feeds in a row → tell the family or the nurse.

🌅 Start of each morning shift

  • Yellow mug: water + ½ pack electrolytes + 1 cap MiraLAX + 1–2 tsp acacia fiber.
  • Big white jug: water + 1.5 packs electrolytes.
  • Goal: about 1,500 ml drink + 60 g protein a day. More drink is better.
  • Pushing protein → watch heart rate, go slow.

🥤 Our cups and jugs (measure once)

  • Yellow mug = 14 oz ≈ 415 ml (full).
  • Big white jug = ______ ml (full) — measure once, write it here.
  • Protein-drink bottle = ______ ml.
  • Use these numbers instead of guessing. "Finished the mug" = 415 ml; "½ the jug" = half its ml.

⏰ Falling behind? Check by mid-afternoon

  • Add up the totals at 3 pm.
  • Has the patient had less than about half the daily goal? Plan extra feeds before bed.
  • Don't wait until night to notice the patient is behind.

💧 Pee colour guide (1–7)

1 — pale, drinking enough
2 — light yellow, OK
3 — yellow, OK
4 — dark yellow, push fluids
5 — amber, tell the nurse
6 — orange-brown, tell the nurse
7 — brown, tell the nurse right away

🦠 Keeping food safe

  • 2-hour rule: don't leave food out at room temperature more than 2 hours (dairy blends, Ensure).
  • 1 hour if the food is warm.
  • Opened Ensure: use or put in the fridge within 4 hours; throw away after 2 days in the fridge.
  • Never serve food the patient already ate from — throw the rest away.
  • Make small portions. One portion = one use. Start a timer when food leaves the fridge or is warmed.

📞 When to call the nurse or doctor

  • Urine colour is 4 or higher and stays dark even after extra fluids.
  • Far fewer wet diapers than her normal, or no real wet diaper in about 8 hours.
  • Any watery diarrhea (especially 3 or more in a day), or any vomiting.
  • She takes half or less of her feeds, or loses more than she drinks.
  • The patient is unable to take their medications by themselves.
  • Other signs of dehydration (see the box below), or the 3 purple blood numbers are dropping.
  • Confusion, racing heart, trouble breathing, or new swelling → call right away.

🩹 Signs of dehydration

  • Urine colour 4 or higher on the chart, or dark / strong-smelling pee — the earliest sign.
  • Fewer wet diapers than her normal.
  • Dry mouth, dry lips, or no tears.
  • Skin pinch: gently pinch the skin on the back of the hand or forearm. If it stays tented and is slow to fall back flat, she may be dehydrated.
  • Sunken eyes, or seeming more tired, foggy, or confused than usual at rest.

Do not sit her up to test for dizziness or a faint pulse — that can trigger a crash. Judge from urine colour, diaper count, and how she looks at rest. A few signs together → push fluids and tell the nurse.

⚖️ Weight — when to call

  • Weigh the patient at the same time of day (best in the morning), in similar clothing, on the same scale.
  • Sudden loss — more than about 2 lb in a day, or 5 lb in a week → tell the nurse. This is often fluid.
  • Steady loss — more than about 5% of body weight in a month (for a 110 lb person, that's about 5–6 lb) → tell the doctor.
  • Any fast, unexplained weight change → call.
Why this matters: when everyone measures and writes things the same way, the notes can be trusted. That helps the nurse and doctor make good decisions for the patient.
