Additional resources

A gathered list — the organizations that specialize in this illness, plus blogs, clinicians, books, and brain-retraining programs that others living with ME/CFS and related illnesses have found worth knowing about. Nothing here is required reading; it's here for the day curiosity returns.

Brain-retraining & nervous-system programs

Neuroplasticity approaches, in order. Most are built around video and audio, so they're for later or for the people helping you to explore.

There is a companion page, Neuroplasticity approaches you can use from bed, that explains the quiet, silent version of these practices — the parts you can do lying down with your eyes closed. The programs below are the fuller courses behind them.

Start here

DNRS — Dynamic Neural Retraining System

The best-known neuroplasticity program for illnesses like this. The course itself is on video, so it's for when watching is possible again — but the practice it teaches is exactly the calm-picturing and interrupt-the-alarm steps in the companion page.

Needs video to start Practice is silent

retrainingthebrain.com/the-program

Most relevant when bedbound

Primal Trust

Cathleen King

Cathleen King had severe ME/CFS herself, and the program speaks directly to people who are bedbound — its whole philosophy is "regulate the nervous system first, and keep every practice tiny." Still video-based, but the spirit matches a hard reality better than most.

Video-based Made for very ill / bedbound

primaltrust.org

Has short audio

Gupta Program

Similar internal approach to DNRS, with some short audio meditations that can be used in small doses on a tolerable day.

Video + short audio

guptaprogram.com

Mostly written

ANS Rewire

A structured program with a strong written component, which can be easier than video when reading in short bursts is possible. Built specifically around ME/CFS and pacing.

Mostly written ME/CFS-specific

cfsrecovery.com.au (ANS Rewire)

Simple app, for later

Curable

A phone app built around short audio exercises and "somatic tracking" — gently noticing sensations with curiosity instead of fear. Designed for pain, but the calming tools transfer. Best for a later stage when a little screen and audio are manageable.

App · short audio

curablehealth.com

Organizations & care guides

The groups that focus on exactly this — severe ME/CFS and the day-to-day of caring for someone living with it. These are the institutions behind much of what's on the other pages here, and good places to go deeper.

Closest to what we've built

Bateman Horne Center — Crash Survival Guidebook

A leading US ME/CFS clinic. Their free guidebook is written for the patient, the people helping, and the care team together — each chapter a standalone handout on living with crashes and PEM. It includes printable communication cards for crash days, which inspired the ones in our own Daily Care Forms.

Free PDF Clinician-made Crash & communication cards

For the bedbound day-to-day

Emerge Australia — Caring for someone who is bedbound

The Australian ME/CFS charity. This page is the most directly on-point guide we found for hands-on home care: bathing, toileting, oral care, dressing, positioning, the sensory room, communication, and looking after the caregiver too.

Practical home care Severe / bedbound

emerge.org.au — caring for someone bedbound

A whole resource library

Solve M.E. — Patient & Caregiver Resources

A large US nonprofit with a deep, well-organized library of free guides — symptom tracking, pacing and PEM, "Loving Someone with ME/CFS," caregiver support and burnout, finding a doctor, and navigating disability. A good place to browse when you want more on any one topic.

Only for the severely affected

25% ME Group

A UK charity exclusively for people with severe and very severe ME — the 25% most affected. They publish a "Caring for Caregivers" resource and a Hospital Support Pack built around the UK care guidelines, a useful companion to our Managed Hospital Admission page.

Blogs, clinicians & books

Where to learn, who to look for, and what to read.

Where these came from

Everything in this section was recommended in a letter from Kristin Luker — Professor of Sociology and of Law, Emerita, at UC Berkeley — written to a friend whose loved one has ME/CFS-like illness. She lives with CFS herself and offers these as a fellow traveler, not as medical advice. Her framing: when an illness sits "at the edge of Western medicine," there are no quick fixes, and the most useful thing is good company and good information.

Understand the key symptom

Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM) — CDC

PEM — the worsening of symptoms after even minor physical or mental exertion — is the classic feature that distinguishes ME/CFS from many other conditions. The CDC's clinical page explains it and the role of activity management (pacing).

Finding care

A "Center of Excellence," or a CFS-literate doctor

Kristin's central advice: try to find a Center of Excellence within reach, or at least a doctor experienced in treating CFS-like illnesses. She also notes she has personally had luck with integrative medicine — though, as always, results vary. (Health Rising, above, keeps a list of doctors and centers.)

From Kristin Luker's letter

Book · living with it

How to Be Sick

Toni Bernhard

A crash course in living with these kinds of illnesses — how to write a "not-to-do" list, how to deal with people who get impatient that you aren't well yet, and those who see you on a good day and assume you're malingering on a bad one. Any of Bernhard's books would serve.

Book · memoir

The Invisible Kingdom

Meghan O'Rourke

A writer's account of years inside an ambiguous chronic illness, and of a medical system poorly built for conditions that don't fit neatly into its categories. (Recommended in the letter as "Invisible Illness.")

From Kristin Luker's letter

Book · memoir

The Deep Places

Ross Douthat

A memoir of chronic illness "at the edge of Western medicine" — the phrase Kristin borrows to describe what it's like to be sick in a way the system can't readily name.

From Kristin Luker's letter

Book · physician's memoir

Brave New Medicine

Cynthia Li, MD

A doctor's account of becoming a patient with an autoimmune/chronic illness, and of finding her way to a different paradigm of healing than the one she was trained in.
