The Care Team — Roles for a Hospital at Home

Core team

Physician / Medical Lead — the ordering doctor

The doctor everything hangs on. Acts as the "admitting" physician for the home setup: writes the orders the rest of the team works under, sets thresholds (how fast to add calories, when to call), and decides hospital-vs-home.

  • Writes orders for nursing, labs, equipment (DME), and the feeding plan.
  • Sets the numeric triggers for when to call or escalate.
  • The point of contact for clinical changes the nurse flags.

Home Health Nurse (RN) — visiting / skilled nurse

The clinical backbone in the home. Works under the doctor's orders, visits on a schedule, and is the eyes-and-ears that catch clinical change early.

  • Assessments and vitals; skin / pressure-injury checks.
  • Blood draws and labs (e.g. the electrolyte panel during refeeding).
  • Medication administration and oversight (including any taper).
  • Teaches the family and aides how to do tasks safely.
  • Flags changes back to the physician.

Registered Dietitian — nutritionist

Owns the feeding and refeeding plan. Decides calorie and fluid targets and — critically — how fast calories are added, the safety question during refeeding.

  • Sets calorie / protein / fluid targets and the blend recipes.
  • Sets the pace of increase to avoid refeeding syndrome.
  • Adjusts the plan as weight and labs trend.

Case Manager — agency / insurance side

Makes the system pay for and approve the care. Usually assigned by the home-health agency or insurer when skilled nursing is ordered. Handles the paperwork layer, not the patient.

  • Authorizations and coverage; what insurance pays for vs. out of pocket.
  • Orders durable medical equipment (e.g. a bed scale) through DME.
  • Arranges the home-health benefit and manages care transitions.

Care Manager / Coordinator — often a family member

Makes the care actually happen well, day to day. The "quarterback" who keeps the whole team in sync. Frequently a family member; can also be hired privately (an independent / professional care manager).

  • Orchestrates the aide schedule and fills gaps.
  • Sits in on appointments, advocates, keeps records in one place.
  • Translates between the clinical team and the household.

Home Health Aides / CNAs — caregivers, personal-care aides

The bulk of the hands-on hours. The rotating people who provide direct daily care and do most of the paper tracking at the bedside.

  • Bathing, hygiene, repositioning / turning, feeding assistance.
  • Fill in the daily paper forms (food & fluid, turns, symptoms).
  • Often the first to notice day-to-day changes.

The short version

A nurse delivers clinical care; a case or care manager coordinates it. One has hands on the patient, the other has hands on the system around the patient.

A case manager makes sure the system approves and pays for the care. A care manager makes sure the care actually happens well — a role a family member often fills.
